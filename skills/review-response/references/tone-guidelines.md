# Tone Guidelines

This document provides tone guidelines for rebuttal writing, helping maintain professional, polite, and persuasive expression.

## Core Principles

### 1. Always Thank

**Principle**: Start every response with thanks, even when disagreeing with the reviewer's opinion.

**Recommended expressions**:
- "We thank the reviewer for..."
- "We appreciate the reviewer's..."
- "We are grateful for..."

**Avoid**:
- Jumping directly into rebuttal
- Skipping the acknowledgment

---

### 2. Maintain Respect

**Principle**: Even when reviewers misunderstand or are mistaken, maintain a respectful attitude.

**Recommended expressions**:
- "We respectfully note that..."
- "We would like to clarify that..."
- "We appreciate the reviewer's perspective, however..."

**Avoid**:
- "The reviewer is wrong"
- "This is obvious"
- "The reviewer misunderstood"
- "The reviewer failed to notice"

---

### 3. Objective Statements

**Principle**: Use objective, fact-based language; avoid emotional expressions.

**Recommended expressions**:
- "Our experiments show that..."
- "The results demonstrate..."
- "Based on our analysis..."

**Avoid**:
- "Obviously..."
- "Clearly..."
- "It is well-known that..."
- "Everyone knows..."


---

### 4. Constructive Responses

**Principle**: Even when disagreeing, provide constructive explanations and alternatives.

**Recommended expressions**:
- "While [reviewer's suggestion] has merit, we chose [our approach] because..."
- "We have considered this approach, however..."
- "An alternative perspective is..."

**Avoid**:
- "This suggestion is not feasible" (without explanation)
- "We cannot do this" (without alternatives)

---

### 5. Acknowledge Shortcomings

**Principle**: When reviewers point out real problems, honestly acknowledge them and describe improvements.

**Recommended expressions**:
- "We acknowledge this limitation..."
- "We agree that this could be improved..."
- "This is a valid concern..."

**Avoid**:
- Evading the issue
- Over-defending
- Deflecting the topic

---

## Professional Expressions

### Expressions of Thanks

| Scenario | Recommended Expression |
|----------|----------------------|
| General thanks | "We thank the reviewer for this comment/suggestion" |
| Emphasizing value | "We thank the reviewer for this valuable/insightful feedback" |
| Important suggestion | "We thank the reviewer for this excellent/important suggestion" |
| Catching an error | "We thank the reviewer for catching this/pointing this out" |


---

### Expressions of Agreement

| Scenario | Recommended Expression |
|----------|----------------------|
| Full agreement | "We agree that..." / "We concur with the reviewer that..." |
| Partial agreement | "We agree that [part], however..." |
| Acknowledging a problem | "We acknowledge this limitation/concern" |
| Accepting a suggestion | "We have adopted this suggestion" |

---

### Expressions of Disagreement (Polite Defense)

| Scenario | Recommended Expression |
|----------|----------------------|
| Polite disagreement | "We respectfully note that..." |
| Providing justification | "However, we chose [approach] because..." |
| Explaining constraints | "While [suggestion] would be valuable, it is not feasible due to..." |
| Offering alternative | "Instead, we have [alternative], which..." |

---

### Clarification Expressions

| Scenario | Recommended Expression |
|----------|----------------------|
| Pointing to existing content | "We would like to clarify that..." |
| Acknowledging unclear writing | "We apologize for the confusion. What we meant is..." |
| Providing location reference | "This is discussed in Section X (page Y)" |
| Improving wording | "To make this clearer, we have..." |


---

### Experimental Commitment Expressions

| Scenario | Recommended Expression |
|----------|----------------------|
| Experiments completed | "We have conducted additional experiments on..." |
| Committing to experiments | "We are currently conducting... and will include the results" |
| Showing results | "The results show that..." / "Our experiments demonstrate..." |
| Experiments not feasible | "While [experiment] would be valuable, it is not feasible due to [constraint]. However, we have [alternative]" |

---

## Tone Adjustments for Different Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Reviewer is completely right

**Tone**: Honestly accept, thank for pointing it out

**Example**:
> "We thank the reviewer for pointing out this important issue. We agree that [problem description]. We have [specific improvement]."

---

### Scenario 2: Reviewer is partially right

**Tone**: Agree with the reasonable part, politely explain the other part

**Example**:
> "We appreciate the reviewer's feedback. We agree that [reasonable part]. However, regarding [other part], we respectfully note that [explanation]."

---

### Scenario 3: Reviewer misunderstood the paper

**Tone**: Polite clarification, acknowledge possible unclear writing

**Example**:
> "We thank the reviewer for this comment. We would like to respectfully clarify that [clarification]. This is discussed in [location]. We apologize if this was not sufficiently clear, and we have revised [location] to make this more explicit."


---

### Scenario 4: Reviewer requests something infeasible

**Tone**: Explain constraints, provide alternatives

**Example**:
> "We appreciate this suggestion. However, [suggested approach] is not feasible in our setting due to [specific constraint]. Instead, we have [alternative], which [result]. We believe this approach [advantage]."

---

### Scenario 5: Multiple reviewers have conflicting opinions

**Tone**: Balance both perspectives, explain the rationale for the choice made

**Example**:
> "We appreciate both Reviewer X's and Reviewer Y's perspectives on [issue]. After careful consideration, we have [our choice] because [reason]. We believe this approach addresses both reviewers' core concerns while [advantage]."

---

## Tone Comparison Examples

### Example 1: Responding to methodology questions

**Bad expression**:
> "The reviewer is wrong. Our method is clearly better than the baseline."

**Good expression**:
> "We appreciate the reviewer's concern about our method choice. We respectfully note that our method is more suitable for this setting because [reason 1] and [reason 2]. Our experiments show that it achieves [specific result]. We have added this discussion to Section 3.2."

**Analysis**: The good expression maintains politeness, provides specific reasons and evidence, and indicates the location of the improvement.


---

### Example 2: Responding to a misunderstanding

**Bad expression**:
> "The reviewer failed to notice that we already did this experiment in Section 4."

**Good expression**:
> "We thank the reviewer for this comment. We would like to respectfully clarify that we did include this experiment in our study. The results are presented in Table 3 (page 6) and discussed in Section 4.2. To make this more prominent, we have added a dedicated paragraph highlighting these findings."

**Analysis**: The good expression avoids blaming the reviewer, politely clarifies, and proactively improves the presentation.

---

### Example 3: Responding to an infeasible suggestion

**Bad expression**:
> "This is impossible. We don't have access to that dataset."

**Good expression**:
> "We appreciate this suggestion. While experiments on dataset X would be valuable, this dataset is not publicly available and requires institutional access that we currently do not have. However, we have conducted experiments on dataset Y, which has similar characteristics. The results show [findings]. We have added these results to Section 4.4."

**Analysis**: The good expression explains the reason for the constraint, provides an alternative, and shows concrete action.


---

### Example 4: Responding to typo corrections

**Bad expression**:
> "Fixed."

**Good expression**:
> "We thank the reviewer for catching this. We have corrected all typos throughout the manuscript."

**Analysis**: Even for a minor typo, express thanks and describe the scope of the correction.

---

### Example 5: Responding to requests for additional experiments

**Bad expression**:
> "We will add these experiments in the final version."

**Good expression**:
> "We thank the reviewer for this excellent suggestion. We have conducted additional experiments comparing our method with baseline X on all three datasets. The results show that our method achieves [specific result]. These new results have been added to Table 4 and discussed in Section 4.5."

**Analysis**: The good expression shows completed work, provides specific results and location, rather than a vague commitment.


---

## Summary: Tone Checklist

After completing the rebuttal, use the following checklist to review the tone:

### Must include

- [ ] Every response begins with thanks
- [ ] Use "We" rather than "I"
- [ ] Provide specific location references (sections, page numbers, tables, figures)
- [ ] Describe specific improvement actions
- [ ] Maintain polite and professional tone

### Must avoid

- [ ] No "The reviewer is wrong" or similar expressions
- [ ] No arrogant words like "Obviously", "Clearly"
- [ ] No defensive or aggressive tone
- [ ] No vague commitments ("We will..." without specifics)
- [ ] No ignoring any review comment


---

## Usage Recommendations

### 1. Writing Process

1. **Draft**: Write out the content of all responses first without over-focusing on tone
2. **Tone check**: Use the checklist in this document to review each response
3. **Replace expressions**: Replace inappropriate expressions with recommended professional ones
4. **Overall review**: Ensure consistent tone across all responses
5. **Peer review**: Ask a colleague or advisor to review whether the tone is appropriate

### 2. Common Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1: Over-defensiveness**
- Problem: Feeling attacked, tone becomes defensive
- Solution: Take a breath; remember that reviewers are helping improve the paper

**Pitfall 2: Over-conceding**
- Problem: Saying "Yes" to everything, losing your position
- Solution: When there are sufficient reasons, defend politely but firmly

**Pitfall 3: Vague responses**
- Problem: Responses are evasive and lack concrete action
- Solution: Every response should have specific Response and Changes sections

### 3. Tone Differences Across Conferences/Journals

**Top conferences (NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR)**:
- Greater focus on technical details
- Can discuss technical choices more directly
- But still maintain politeness

**Top journals (Nature, Science)**:
- Greater focus on broader impact
- Tone needs to be more careful and comprehensive
- Emphasize social significance and impact

**Domain conferences**:
- Adjust to field culture
- Some fields are more direct, others more reserved

### 4. Final Reminder

**Remember**: Reviewers are volunteers; their time spent reviewing your paper is meant to help you. Even if you disagree with their opinions, express gratitude and respect.

**Goal**: Make reviewers feel:
- Their comments were taken seriously
- The authors made substantive improvements
- The authors have a professional, cooperative attitude

This kind of rebuttal is most likely to receive a positive response.
