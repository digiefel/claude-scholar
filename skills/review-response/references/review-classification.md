# Review Comment Classification Criteria

This document provides a systematic method for classifying review comments, helping to quickly identify and prioritize different types of comments.

## Classification System

### 1. Major Issues

**Definition**: Core problems related to research methodology, experimental design, result interpretation, etc., requiring substantial revisions or additional experiments.

**Characteristics**:
- Questioning the validity of the research method
- Requiring supplementary key experiments or comparisons
- Pointing out logical problems in result interpretation
- Questioning the novelty or contribution of the research

**Keywords to identify**:
- "major concern"
- "fundamental issue"
- "missing experiments"
- "insufficient evidence"
- "not convincing"


**Example**:
> "The authors should compare their method with recent work [X]. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the true contribution."

**Recommended response strategy**: Accept + Experiment

---

### 2. Minor Issues

**Definition**: Problems requiring clarification, supplementary explanation, or small revisions, not affecting the core contribution of the research.

**Characteristics**:
- Requesting clarification of certain details
- Suggesting improvements to wording or organization
- Requesting supplementary discussion of related work
- Pointing out that figures or tables could be improved

**Keywords to identify**:
- "minor concern"
- "could be improved"
- "please clarify"
- "it would be better"
- "suggestion"

**Example**:
> "The notation in Section 3.2 is confusing. Please clarify the meaning of variable α."

**Recommended response strategy**: Accept + Clarify


---

### 3. Typos/Formatting

**Definition**: Surface-level issues such as spelling errors, grammar problems, inconsistent formatting.

**Characteristics**:
- Pointing out spelling errors
- Noting grammar issues
- Pointing out inconsistent citation format
- Errors in figure/table labels

**Keywords to identify**:
- "typo"
- "grammar"
- "formatting"
- "inconsistent"
- "Figure X should be"

**Example**:
> "Page 5, line 3: 'recieve' should be 'receive'."

**Recommended response strategy**: Accept (directly correct)


---

### 4. Misunderstandings

**Definition**: Misreadings or misinterpretations of the paper by the reviewer, requiring polite clarification.

**Characteristics**:
- Reviewer misunderstood the method or results
- Reviewer overlooked existing explanations in the paper
- Reviewer has an incorrect understanding of a concept

**Keywords to identify**:
- "The authors did not..." (but it was already done)
- "It is unclear..." (but it was already explained)
- "Why don't the authors..." (but it was already done)

**Example**:
> "The authors did not compare with baseline X."
> (But Table 2 in the paper already includes this comparison)

**Recommended response strategy**: Clarify (politely point to existing content in the paper)


---

## Usage Guide

### Classification Workflow

1. **First pass** - Quickly scan all review comments, mark keywords
2. **Classify** - Label each comment by type (Major/Minor/Typo/Misunderstanding)
3. **Prioritize** - Sort by importance (Major > Misunderstanding > Minor > Typo)
4. **Plan strategy** - Determine a response strategy for each comment

### Priority Recommendations

**High priority** (must handle first):
- Major Issues - require additional experiments or major revisions
- Misunderstandings - require clarification to prevent deepening of misunderstanding

**Medium priority**:
- Minor Issues - need improvement but do not affect core contribution

**Low priority**:
- Typos/Formatting - directly correct

### Notes

- **Do not ignore any comment** - Even a minor typo should be acknowledged in the rebuttal
- **Be polite about misunderstandings** - Use "We apologize for the confusion" rather than "The reviewer misunderstood"
- **Major Issues require substantive responses** - Cannot just clarify; action is needed (experiments, revisions, etc.)
